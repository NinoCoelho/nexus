## When to use

- Reviewing articles before publication
- Editing submissions for clarity and impact
- Providing editorial feedback on drafts
- Assessing journalistic quality of news or opinion pieces
- Debugging why a piece isn't working
- Prefer over generic "check this text" requests when the goal is professional editorial standards

## Steps

### 1. First Pass -- The 30-Second Scan

Read once through quickly. Note:
- Does the piece have a clear point?
- Do you know what the story is about by paragraph 3?
- Does the ending connect to the beginning?
- Any immediate red flags (confusing sections, dead ends)

**If you can't answer "what is this about?" after one read, the structure fails.**

### 2. Structural Review

Check these in order:

| Attribute | What to Look For | Pass/Fail Threshold |
|-----------|------------------|---------------------|
| **Hook/Lead** | First paragraph grabs attention without being clickbaity; signals what follows | Clear promise delivered within 5 paragraphs |
| **Narrative Arc** | Has beginning, middle, end; tension and resolution | Logical progression, no abrupt jumps |
| **Paragraph Flow** | Each paragraph follows from the one before; transitions work | No "where did this come from?" moments |
| **Pacing** | Information released at right speed; not dumping, not dragging | Momentum maintained throughout |
| **Ending** | Lands the plane; doesn't trail off or abruptly stop | Last paragraph feels earned |

### 3. Substantive Review

| Attribute | What to Look For | Pass/Fail Threshold |
|-----------|------------------|---------------------|
| **Clarity of Argument** (opinion/analysis) | Thesis is explicit; every paragraph serves it | Reader can state the argument back in one sentence |
| **Evidence Base** (news/reporting) | Claims are supported by facts, quotes, data; sources are credible | No unsubstantiated assertions |
| **Fairness** | Multiple viewpoints represented when appropriate; avoids straw men | Opposing view given genuine treatment |
| **Precision** | No weasel words ("many people say", "experts believe") without attribution | Every claim has a source or qualifier |
| **Originality** | Says something new; doesn't repeat what's already known | At least one insight not obvious from the facts |

### 4. Stylistic Review

| Attribute | What to Look For | Pass/Fail Threshold |
|-----------|------------------|---------------------|
| **Sentence Variety** | Mix of lengths and structures; no repetitive patterns | No three consecutive sentences of same length/structure |
| **Jargon Control** | Technical terms explained or avoided | Reader outside field can follow |
| **Active Voice** | Passive voice used only when intentional | >80% active verbs (except in news leads for attribution) |
| **Adjective Loading** | Descriptive language used precisely, not as filler | No strings of stacked modifiers |
| **Cliche Avoidance** | No overused phrases ("game-changer", "paradigm shift", "in this day and age") | Fresh language throughout |

### 5. Mechanics Review

| Attribute | What to Look For |
|-----------|------------------|
| **Grammar** | Standard English rules followed |
| **Usage** | Common errors checked (affect/effect, who/whom, imply/infer) |
| **Punctuation** | Correct usage; consistent style |
| **Attribution** | Quotes introduced correctly; speaker clear |
| **Facts** | Names, dates, numbers, titles verified |

### 6. The "So What?" Test

After reading, ask:
- Why does this piece exist?
- What does the reader walk away with?
- Is the point worth the words?

**If the answer is unclear, the piece needs tightening or reframing.**

### 7. Provide Feedback Structure

When delivering editorial review, use this format:

#### Summary
One-sentence verdict: Publish/Needs work/Reject

#### Strengths
- [What works]

#### Critical Issues
1. **[Category]** - [Specific issue] - [Why it matters] - [How to fix]

#### Minor Issues
- [Optional polish points]

#### Verdict
[Publish / Revise and resubmit / Major rewrite needed]

### 8. Genre-Specific Standards

**News/Reporting:**
- Lead paragraph answers: Who, what, when, where, why
- Attribution on every claim
- No editorializing in news voice
- Multiple sources for claims of fact

**Opinion/Editorial:**
- Argument stated upfront
- Evidence supports, doesn't just assert
- Acknowledges counterarguments fairly
- Tone matches publication voice

**Feature/Narrative:**
- Scene-setting with specific detail
- Character development (if applicable)
- Narrative tension sustained
- Universal insight from specific story

**Analysis/Explainer:**
- Complexity respected, not oversimplified
- Jargon explained on first use
- Examples clarify, not decorate
- Structure scaffolds understanding

### 9. Common Patterns to Catch

**Red Flags:**
- "There are many reasons why..." -> Which ones? Be specific.
- "It is important to note that..." -> Cut. If it's important, just say it.
- "In conclusion..." -> Let the conclusion land on its own.
- "First, second, third..." when not actually listing things
- Paragraphs that start with "However" or "But" repeatedly

**Better:**
- Delete throat-clearing openers
- Combine short, choppy paragraphs into developed units
- Break long, wandering paragraphs into focused units
- Replace "there is/there are" with active constructions
- Delete every sentence that doesn't advance the piece

## Gotchas

- Don't rewrite the author's voice; fix problems, don't change personality
- Distinguish between stylistic preference and objective flaw
- Consider publication context and audience (what's wrong for NYT may be fine for a blog)
- Don't let perfect be enemy of good; flag fatal errors, suggest polish on minor issues
- For non-native English speakers, separate language mechanics from structural thinking
- Always flag factual errors before stylistic issues
- When in doubt about a fact, flag for verification rather than guessing
